Going in and going out of Cambodia. Whatever happens in between.

April 04, 2017

Because you might find this story funny, here how I entered Cambodia, how I let myself guided by the events and how I succeeded to leave the country.

Here we are, Ho Chi Minh. This day was my last in Vietnam, as my visa would expire the day after. I had just leaved a group of fellow backpackers I spend time with from Phong Nah to Hoi An. Alone again, I jumped in the bus who would take me to Phnom Penh, Cambodia.
I knew they would ask me to pay 35 bucks for my visa at the border, when it should have been only 30. I had planned not to play the game of corruption, and when the driver ask the bunch of foreigner from the bus the 35, here I stand, claiming that I won’t let that happen. With the consent of the backpacker team, 30 it will be, even if we have to wait one hour at the border.
Meanwhile the bus broke down, forcing us to wait for another one in the middle of the highway, but our determination was still intact. Until the driver hand me this mad Vietnamese lady on the phone, who made clear that if we wanted to pay 30 bucks, they will do whatever would be possible to make it painful. Meaning paying night time fee at the border, waiting one hour, and using bikes to catch the bus, who won’t be waiting for us but five miles from the border.
We had the choice of eventually paying the same price in both case, so it was just a matter of loosing either time or self-esteem.
We crossed the border in ten minutes.

Arriving in Phnom Penh, I headed to my hostel, with Angèle, nerdy French girl, with who I would’ve probably not talk to if it wasn’t for all our bus troubles.
She didn’t knew where to sleep so I was happy to lead her to the hostel, as well as making a new friend before even setting foot in Cambodia.

The next few days, still strained by the aborted revolution from the bus earlier, I was willing to forget about making any plan for a while, letting Angèle and her strong and effective leadership driving us around.

Not that I don’t feel bad about letting other people do all the searching and planning job though. This is more like letting randomness - in that case other people decisions - choose my path. Raven and Lief, met in Sihanoukville, would have used dices to make decisions while traveling together. Should be fun to try.

So, chilling by the swimming pool in Phnom Penh - 6$ Hostel have swimming pool there, why not - Angèle start speaking about her American friend doing a work-away in Kampot, south of Cambodia, and that we could met her as there’s many thing to see there. Still not willing to make any big decision of my own for a while, I accept to take the bus to this funny named city.
That’s actually a weird but enjoyable feeling, going to a place you never heard about, in a country you know nothing about - I hadn’t open a book or read on the web about Cambodia at that time. You should try it, let’s call it a blind trip.

Kampot here I am, with Angèle and another solo backpacker named Kareen, who will unfortunately be forced to leave us after she got quite sick from the traitorous sun of Cambodia.

Our stay in this small but charming city went on, between paddle boarding, bikes trip to the mountain, local food and a couple beers. I liked the group I was with, Angèle, Madison, Lauren and other passing-by faces. We eventually left this place with Angèle for the beaches and island of Sihanoukville. Maddie would join us a couple days after, and I was glad this chain of events made me traveling with these two awesome human being.

Sun, beaches. I was still letting myself driven by the waves of surrounding friends. That’s how I got drunk with people worth to meet, resulting in beer-pong games, dancing and midnight swimming.

The next morning, hangover, we would take a early - too early - boat to Koh Rong Sanloem, heavenly island where we would enjoy a night in a cozy bungalow by the sea.

Angèle, Maddie and me were now ready to go north, and after a short stay at Battambank, the city of the five million bats, with vivid memories of our trip in the Battuktuk, we reached the city of Siem Reap, were we would stay a couple of days, before everyone would make their own way in different country and the crew would eventually split.

So, why telling all this story, when I usually find that writing about my travel stories is boring ? Well to make a statement I guess, that the saying “fortune favours the bold” hadn’t apply to me so far. I tried a different way of traveling which happened to be successful in many ways.

That could be a attempt to answer to a question we, backpackers and vagabond, ask each other every day: “So, what do you advice me to do in X country”. And the answer to be extremely different each time you ask a new person. While one might have loved a certain place, another would tell you there’s nothing to do there.
Which isn’t a surprise at all, you don’t have the same expectation, weather, people to hang out with, so the place itself isn’t enough to make an objective review.
Now, you’re like: “So, where the fuck am I going then? What will be the best for me?“. And most of the time, you can’t tell. More reason to make random choices then, no?
The cool thing, is that you will have no way to know if you made the right call going to that island rather than this other one, because you didn’t go to the latter.
Rather than hoping to be lucky on your trip, tell yourself you were so far. You can’t prove or deny it, hence a win-win for you.

Furthermore, and I’m not the first to say it, the things you remember, enjoy afterward and tell to peoples are either trouble, bad things, being sick, loosing stuff, walking under the sun for hours or to the other extreme unexpected events which belong to you and you alone.
And you can’t plan this.

In the end, without planning, without expectations, driven by my feeling in the instant, I met wonderful people, among them Maddie, a huge smilestone in this trip in Khmer country, and someone I will hopefully meat again.

Now is time to leave Cambodia, and reach Bangkok. To do so, let’s book a night bus !
Alone again, after a few beers for courage and several pool games against locals - I’m improving a lot since I’m in East Asia, I’m waiting for the bus pickup. Who’s late, of course.
When it finally comes, I need to hurry, of course, and quickly hop in the bus.
Roughly half a hour after the bus left, comfortably lying in my bunk, a guy came and ask me if I’m going to Bangkok, and positive is my answer. His answer however, not so, for this bus is going back to Phnom Penh. Having already visited this city, I wasn’t really looking forward to go back there, I had Thai projects in mind instead.
Surprisingly chill despite the late 2am-ish hour, I waited on the front of the bus for them to drop me and caught another bus going back to Siem Reap, in which I chatted a bit with a French-speaking Cambodian.
Back in Siem Reap, I waited again, occasionally exchanging a few words with other travelers passing-by.
Eventually, here’s a bus to bangkok, I double-checked, I’ll make it this time.
After one of the longest border crossing I’ve experienced, I’m able to see the tall buildings of Bangkok, at last. For a trip that should have taken height hours, sixteen it took. But that was less boring than regular without-pitfall bus trip.
Make mistakes while traveling, it adds up to the experience in a good way.
